Software Conservatism

C/U/ID: 2023-06-02/2023-06-03/P003

Background

Recently, instant messaging social platform Discord announced that they are changing the way usernames work on their platform. They cited overcomplexity in their current system and a desire for a "robust long-term solution" as the main reasons for the change. The new system will work a lot like Twitter, with each user having a unique, lowercase, and (mostly) alphanumeric username prefixed with an @ and a non-unique display name that can be changed at will and will be what everyone actually sees (not unlike their current server nicknames system, except platform-wide).

To me, at least, this seems at worst an inane but harmless change arisen from misplaced priorities. Most people will go from user "FooBar#1234" with display name "FooBar" to user "foobar1" with username "FooBar". No difference on the frontend. If a person is intent on using the 4 numbers, they could use "foobar1234" as their username. Functionally identical.

However, instead of the expected "oh okay" from users, I (not anecdotally) saw extreme opposition to this change. Users complained about Discord "becoming more like other social medias", the potential for account selling, that this system is somehow more complex than the discriminator--all somewhat valid arguments, but (in my opinion) not enough to warrant the extreme backlash hurled at this change. Importantly, I don't think I've ever seen people argue the other way--that other social medias should adopt Discord's discriminator system. I think the backlash to this change comes from another place--what I call 'software conservatism'.

Software conservatism?

Nowadays, people often associate the word 'conservatism' with politics--an ideology espousing staunch resistance to social or economic reform. However, it's also very applicable in the online space when it comes to software. Many people, even political progressives, have this unreasonable expectation that software should never change in design or function and that one should never need or want to switch from one software to another. I suspect a large part of it is laze--many people have gotten used to opening the same app every day and being spoon-fed content, which I'll admit is much easier than switching to something else and for most people is probably sufficient for what they want. However, when change inevitably comes to their preferred content feeder, they see it as an attack on the userbase itself, even when it's a minor design change. This is the exact same mindset that political conservatives have, and it's a dangerous mindset because it entrenches itself in the believer. It's much easier to be stubborn and unchanging than it is to adapt to societal changes. To be a progressive, you have to be (at least nominally) proactive.

This applies to software and the internet as well. To be an adopter, you have to be an adapter. Technology is progressing faster than ever, and often not in a good way. Getting tripped up over small changes like Discord changing its username system is quite frankly frivolous and a waste of time.

There are many wonderful technologies and platforms developed all the time, but people want to stick with their file formats from the 90s and social medias they'd be better off leaving even though in many cases it would be very easy and a great improvement if they just took the few minutes to switch.
